Rotary Leadership Training

AI Starters for Running a Better Club

A handful of ready-to-use starting points for working with AI tools like Claude, Gemini, and NotebookLM. Find your challenge, copy a starter, add your club's details, and go.

These aren't magic phrases. Each one teaches the same core skill: give the AI real context, ask it to question you before it answers, and expect depth instead of a checklist.

Start Here

New to this? These three teach the whole game. Try them in order.

Introduce Your Club to the AI

I lead a Rotary club and I'm going to ask you for help with a few things today. First, let me give you context so your help is actually specific to us. We're the Rotary Club of [Club Name] in [City], with about [number] members. We meet [day, time, location]. Our members are mostly [describe — age range, professions, how long they've been Rotarians]. The service work we're best known for is [describe]. The thing we most want to improve this year is [describe]. Read all of that back to me in your own words so I know you understand my club, and tell me one thing you'd want to know more about before advising me.

Customize: Fill in every bracket honestly — vague context gets vague answers. Keep this paragraph somewhere; you can paste it at the top of any other starter on this page.

What it teachesThe AI knows nothing about your club until you tell it. Context is the single biggest lever you have.

Ask Before You Answer

I want help with [describe your task in one sentence — e.g., "writing a thank-you note to a sponsor" or "planning next month's program"]. Before you give me anything, ask me the three or four questions that would let you do this really well instead of generically. Wait for my answers, then help me.

Customize: Use this whenever you'd normally just fire off a one-line request. The questions it asks are often more useful than the answer you would have gotten.

What it teachesA good first move is making the AI interview you. It surfaces the details you forgot were important.

Go Deeper Than the First Answer

That's a solid start, but it feels like the safe, generic answer. I want you to push harder. Tell me what I'm probably overlooking, where this could go wrong, and what a more ambitious version would look like. Be direct with me — I'd rather hear a hard truth now than discover it later.

Customize: Paste this as a follow-up after the AI gives you a first response to anything. Repeat it as many times as you like.

What it teachesThe first answer is rarely the best one. Asking for pushback and depth is how you get past the obvious.

Running Your Club

Planning, board meetings, officer transitions, governance

 +

Build a Three-Year Action Plan

I'm the [incoming/current] president of [Club Name], a Rotary club of approximately [number] members in [city/community]. Before we build a three-year strategic plan together, I want you to interview me first — ask the questions a strategic advisor would ask before making any recommendations. Be direct if something I describe sounds like a structural weakness or a gap we're avoiding. Once you've asked your questions and I've answered, help me build a plan that reflects my specific club's situation — not a generic Rotary template.

Customize: Have your last year's numbers handy — membership, finances, event turnout — so you can answer its questions with specifics.

What it teachesGive the AI your role and context first. Ask it to question you before advising you. Expect depth, not a checklist.

Solve a Recurring President-Elect Gap

I'm the current president of the Rotary Club of [Club Name] in [City]. We have [number] members and have been operating for [number] years. We face a recurring problem: identifying and developing a willing president-elect. This happens year after year, and we've tried [briefly describe 1-2 attempts, e.g., "asking at board meetings" or "reaching out to newer members"]. I want you to act as an organizational advisor — not just give me ideas, but help me understand what's likely causing this pattern and what a real structural fix looks like. Challenge me if my description of the problem sounds like I'm missing something. Start by asking me two or three questions so you understand our club before you recommend anything.

Customize: Name what you've already tried, honestly. That's what stops the AI from suggesting the obvious things you've already done.

What it teachesName the pattern, not just the symptom. Give the AI a role (advisor, not answer machine). Ask it to push back and probe before it speaks.

Plan a Board Meeting Worth Attending

I'm planning the agenda for our next board meeting at the Rotary Club of [Club Name]. Here's what's on my mind: [list the topics, decisions, and problems, even messily]. We have [length, e.g., 60 minutes] and our meetings tend to [describe the usual problem — e.g., "run long," "rehash old debates," or "avoid the hard decisions"]. Help me turn this into a tight agenda with clear time blocks and a decision or owner attached to each item. Before you do, ask me which one or two items actually matter most, and flag anything here that looks like a conversation we keep having without resolving.

Customize: Dump your raw notes in — the AI is good at turning a messy brain-dump into a structured agenda. Add your meeting length so the time blocks are realistic.

What it teachesYou don't need a clean request. Hand over the mess and ask the AI to impose structure — and to name the unresolved loop.

Hand Off a Role Without Losing the Knowledge

I'm the outgoing [role — e.g., secretary, treasurer, membership chair] for the Rotary Club of [Club Name], handing off to someone new. So much of what I do lives in my head. I want to build a handoff document that actually sets my successor up to succeed. Interview me: ask the questions that will pull out the recurring tasks, the deadlines, the relationships, and the "here's what nobody tells you" details that I'd otherwise forget to write down. Then organize my answers into a clear handoff guide.

Customize: Do this as a conversation over coffee — answer its questions out loud and paste them back in. The "what nobody tells you" details are the real value.

What it teachesThe AI is excellent at interviewing knowledge out of your head and organizing it — far better than facing a blank document alone.

Growing Your Membership

Attraction, retention, younger members, lapsed members

 +

Attract Younger Members — Honestly

Our club has been working to attract members under 40 for the past [number] years with limited success. Here's what we've already tried: [describe 1-2 efforts, e.g., "we hosted a happy hour event" or "we promoted on Instagram"]. For context: our club meets [day/time/location], our average member age is around [number], and our most visible service project is [describe]. I want you to act as a frank membership strategist — not someone who validates our efforts, but someone who tells me honestly what might be working against us and what we might be missing. Before giving recommendations, ask me what else you'd need to know to make this advice actually specific to our club, not generic.

Customize: Be specific about meeting time, average age, and signature project — those three facts are usually what make or break appeal to younger people.

What it teachesTell the AI what you've tried — that changes everything it suggests. Ask for an honest advisor, not an encourager.

Win Back Members Who Drifted Away

Over the past few years we've lost members at the Rotary Club of [Club Name] — not through conflict, mostly people who quietly drifted off. A few I'd love to bring back are [describe a couple, e.g., "a former board member whose job got busy" or "a younger member who came twice and stopped"]. I want to reach out, but I don't want it to feel like a guilt trip or a renewal pitch. Help me think through why people actually drift, then help me draft a genuine, low-pressure outreach for one specific person once you've asked me a bit about them.

Customize: Pick one real person and answer the AI's questions about them. A message written for one person beats a generic "we miss you" blast every time.

What it teachesSpecificity beats scale. Narrowing to one real person produces a message that sounds human instead of like a form letter.

Design a Retention Check-In

I want to get ahead of member attrition at the Rotary Club of [Club Name] instead of reacting to it. I'm thinking about doing regular check-ins with members, but I don't want it to feel like a survey or HR exercise. Act as a membership advisor: before suggesting anything, ask me about our club's size, culture, and how connected members currently feel. Then help me design a simple, repeatable way to take the pulse of our members and catch the ones who are quietly disengaging — and tell me honestly if a formal check-in is even the right tool here.

Customize: Tell it your real club culture — a 20-person club and a 120-person club need completely different approaches.

What it teachesLet the AI challenge your proposed solution. Sometimes the best answer is "the tool you're reaching for isn't the right one."

Make a First-Time Guest Want to Come Back

We get guests and prospective members at the Rotary Club of [Club Name], but too many come once and never return. Here's what a typical first visit looks like for them right now: [walk through it — how they're greeted, where they sit, what happens during the meeting]. I want you to play the role of a first-time guest and tell me, step by step, where this experience would lose you or make you feel like an outsider. Then help me redesign the guest experience so a newcomer leaves wanting to come back.

Customize: Describe the visit in real detail — the more honestly you narrate it, the sharper the AI can be about the awkward moments you've stopped noticing.

What it teachesAsking the AI to role-play a perspective (here, the nervous newcomer) reveals blind spots you're too close to see.

Events & Fundraising

Planning, promotion, volunteer coordination, post-event wrap-up

 +

Reinvent a Tired Legacy Fundraiser

Our club has run an annual [describe the event — dinner, auction, golf tournament, etc.] for over 20 years. It's been our biggest fundraiser and honestly part of our club's identity in the community — people show up because of what it represents, not just what it raises. The team who built it is now in their mid-70s and ready to step back. We need to either redesign it so it requires far less physical effort, or create something new that can match its community impact and revenue. Before we brainstorm options, ask me the questions that will help you understand what's made this event work — not just logistically, but why people keep coming back. And push back on any assumptions I might be making about what "less strenuous" has to mean — I may be limiting the options without realizing it.

Customize: Give it the emotional context — the traditions, the regulars, what the event means in town — not just the logistics.

What it teachesAsk the AI to uncover why something works before replacing it. Invite it to challenge the constraints you've quietly set.

Plan a New Fundraising Event From Scratch

We want to launch a new fundraising event at the Rotary Club of [Club Name]. Our goals are to raise around [amount/range] and to [secondary goal — e.g., "raise our visibility with younger families" or "involve members who don't usually volunteer"]. For context, our club is [size], our community is [describe], and our volunteer capacity is realistically [be honest]. Don't jump to ideas yet. First, interview me about our resources, our community, and what's worked or flopped before. Then propose a few genuinely different directions — and for each, be upfront about the effort and risk involved.

Customize: Be honest about volunteer capacity. An ambitious event the club can't staff is worse than a modest one it can.

What it teachesState your goals and your real constraints. Ask for distinct options with honest effort/risk labels, not a single "best" idea.

Coordinate Volunteers Without the Chaos

We have an event coming up: [describe the event, date, and rough size]. We'll need volunteers for [list the jobs you can think of]. Every year this part turns into last-minute scrambling and a few people doing everything. Help me build a clear volunteer plan: what roles we actually need, how many people per role, a simple sign-up approach, and what each volunteer should know before they show up. Before you draft it, ask me where things have broken down in the past so we fix the real problem, not just make another spreadsheet.

Customize: Tell it where coordination broke down last time — that's the difference between a generic plan and one that fixes your actual pain.

What it teachesPoint the AI at the real failure point. "Make me a plan" is fine; "fix what keeps breaking" is better.

Run a Post-Event Debrief That Sticks

We just wrapped our [event name]. Here's how it went, the good and the bad: [brain-dump everything — turnout, money raised, what went smoothly, what was stressful, comments you heard]. Help me turn this into a short, useful debrief document that next year's organizers will actually thank us for. Pull out what worked, what to change, and the specific lessons that would otherwise be forgotten by the time we do this again. Ask me anything you need to make it genuinely useful a year from now.

Customize: Do this within a week of the event while details are fresh. Dump everything in — the AI will sort the signal from the venting.

What it teachesThe AI turns scattered recollection into institutional memory — the kind of document clubs almost never get around to writing.

Club Communications

Newsletters, social media, member emails, press releases

 +

Turn Rough Notes Into a Monthly Newsletter

I need to put together this month's newsletter for the Rotary Club of [Club Name]. Here are my raw notes on what happened and what's coming up: [paste everything — bullet points, half-sentences, dates, names]. Our newsletter should sound [describe the tone — warm, brief, a little funny, etc.] and our readers are [describe — busy members, the wider community, both]. Turn this into a clear, friendly newsletter with good section headings and a short, engaging intro. Ask me if anything in my notes is unclear before you write it up.

Customize: Don't polish your notes first — messy bullets are exactly what the AI is good at shaping. Do specify the tone you want.

What it teachesDescribe the tone and the audience. The same facts become a very different newsletter depending on who it's for.

Write a Press Release for a Service Project

Our club just completed (or is about to launch) a service project: [describe what, where, when, who it helps, and any partners or sponsors]. I want a press release we can send to local media. For context, our local outlets are [describe — small-town paper, regional TV, community Facebook groups]. Write it in proper press-release format with a strong headline and a quote I can attribute to [name/role]. Before you write, ask me for the one detail about this project that makes it genuinely newsworthy — I may be burying the most interesting part.

Customize: Have the who/what/where/when ready. Let the AI ask what's newsworthy — clubs often lead with the routine and bury the human story.

What it teachesThe AI can help you find the angle, not just format the facts. Ask it what's actually newsworthy here.

Turn One Event Into a Week of Social Posts

We have an event coming up (or just finished): [describe it]. We post on [platforms — Facebook, Instagram, etc.] and our followers are mostly [describe]. Help me plan a short series of posts — a couple before to build interest, one during, and one after to celebrate and thank people. Keep the tone [describe]. Give me the actual post text plus a note on what photo or visual would go with each. Ask me what matters most about this event before you start, so the posts lead with the right thing.

Customize: Name your platforms — a Facebook post for longtime members reads differently from an Instagram post aimed at newcomers.

What it teachesOne ask can produce a whole sequence. Think in campaigns (before / during / after), not single posts.

Write a Member Email People Actually Open

I need to email our members about [the purpose — a call for volunteers, a schedule change, a dues reminder, etc.]. Here's what they need to know: [the key facts]. Honestly, our members skim these, and I want this one to land. Write me a short email with a subject line that earns the open, a clear ask, and an easy next step. Then give me a one-line alternative subject so I can choose. Tell me if I'm trying to cram in too much — if so, what should I cut?

Customize: If you're tempted to cover five things, let the AI tell you which one to lead with. One clear ask per email gets results.

What it teachesInvite the AI to cut your message down. Editing for focus is something it does well — and something we resist doing ourselves.

Taking It Further

Chaining outputs, using your own documents, going deeper

 +

Feed the AI Your Own Documents

I'm going to share some of our club's own material with you: [describe what you're about to paste or upload — last year's budget, your bylaws, meeting minutes, a past grant application, etc.]. I want your help to [your goal — e.g., "summarize this for new board members," "find inconsistencies," or "draft this year's version"]. Read it carefully first, then before you do the task, tell me what you notice and ask me anything that's unclear or seems to be missing.

Customize: Paste the text directly, or upload the file if your tool allows it. Tools like NotebookLM are built specifically for working from your own documents. Never paste anything you wouldn't want shared.

What it teachesThe AI is far more useful working from your real documents than from generic knowledge. Your material is the context.

Chain One Output Into the Next

We just worked out [the result you reached — e.g., a three-year plan, a new event concept, a membership strategy]. Now I want to put it to work. Using everything we just decided, help me create [the next thing — e.g., "a one-page summary for the board," "a checklist for the event committee," or "talking points for our next meeting"]. Keep it consistent with what we already agreed, and flag anything in the plan that becomes a problem once we try to put it into action.

Customize: Use this in the same conversation, right after finishing something. The AI still remembers the details, so you don't have to re-explain.

What it teachesOne conversation can produce many outputs. A plan becomes a summary becomes a checklist becomes talking points — no starting over.

Build a Reusable Club Context Block

Help me write a reusable "about our club" paragraph that I can paste at the start of any future request to give you instant context. Interview me to pull out the details that matter most — our size, location, culture, strengths, challenges, and the kind of help I'll most often want. Then write it as a tight paragraph I can save and reuse, and tell me what to update over time so it stays accurate.

Customize: Save the result in a notes app. Pasting it at the top of any starter on this page means you never have to re-explain your club again.

What it teachesGood context is reusable. Build it once, and every future conversation starts smarter.

Pressure-Test a Decision Before You Make It

Our board is leaning toward [the decision — e.g., "moving our meeting to evenings," "dropping a long-running project," "raising dues"]. Here's our reasoning: [explain it]. Before we commit, I want you to argue the other side as honestly and forcefully as you can. What are the strongest objections? Who might we be ignoring? What's the failure case we're not talking about? After you've made the counter-argument, give me your genuine assessment — not just both sides, but what you'd actually do.

Customize: Lay out your reasoning fully so the AI has something real to argue against. The point is to hear the case before you vote, not after.

What it teachesAsk the AI to argue against you on purpose. A willing devil's advocate is one of the most valuable things it can be.
